## The Plains of Cannae

August 2, 216 BC. The plains of Cannae. Eighty thousand Roman legionnaires pushed forward, convinced they were winning — the enemy's center was retreating. No one realized this was exactly what Hannibal had designed. The moment Carthaginian cavalry closed in from the rear, the Romans found themselves compressed into an ever-shrinking space. In the end, they couldn't even draw their swords.

In 2025, something similar is playing out across corporations worldwide. Only this time, the upper jaw is the CEO's layoff mandate, the lower jaw is frontline employees secretly using ChatGPT, and the ones crushed in the middle — unable to even draw their swords — are middle managers.

Unlike Cannae, this pincer movement has no commander.

---

## From the Boardroom to the Trenches

CEOs are pushing AI. That's not news. What's news is how they're pushing it — not like advocates, but like people being chased by debt collectors.

A 2025 survey by Writer and Workplace Intelligence found that 42% of C-suite executives admitted AI adoption is "tearing the company apart" [1]. But that didn't slow them down. In the same year, 67% of executives said they were accelerating their AI strategy, up 16 percentage points year-over-year. 61% of companies had already appointed a Chief AI Officer [3].

Why push forward when you know it's causing fractures? Because the consequences of not pushing are more terrifying. McKinsey's report noted that executives were more than twice as likely to view employee readiness as the barrier than to acknowledge their own leadership gaps [2]. Translation: they'd rather say "the people below aren't ready" than admit "I haven't figured this out yet." In front of the board, hesitation is more fatal than failure.

Satya Nadella wrote in a July 2025 internal memo: "Success is not about longevity, but about relevance" [4]. It was an ultimatum. He spoke directly to executives, demanding they commit to transformation or leave. That same month, he announced that 20–30% of Microsoft's code was being written by AI, repositioning the company from "software factory" to "intelligence engine." Roughly 15,000 people were laid off over the course of the year.

Andy Jassy went further. In October 2025, Amazon announced its largest-ever layoffs, cutting 14,000 corporate positions [5]. Beth Galetti's internal memo was unambiguous: "This generation of AI is the most transformative technology we've seen since the internet." Jassy himself was more direct — he believed generative AI could take over many coordination, reporting, and decision-making functions. Those three things happen to be the reason middle management exists.

A similar script played out repeatedly throughout 2025: Benioff openly said on a podcast "I cut customer service from 9,000 to 5,000 people" [6], IBM replaced hundreds of HR employees with AI [7], and Accenture laid off 11,000 while declaring "employees who cannot be retrained will be exited" [8].

Notice the pattern. Not a single CEO said "after careful deliberation, we decided to restructure the organization with AI." Their language was all urgency — "most transformative technology," "commit to transformation or leave," "cannot be retrained, will be exited." Put plainly, these are people being chased by their boards, by capital markets, by each other's layoff numbers. 72% of executives admitted their AI applications were being developed in silos [1] — they didn't even know what their own company's AI was doing, but that didn't stop them from using headcount reductions to prove they were transforming.

This is the upper jaw. Armed with KPIs and timelines, but even those driving it don't know what the full picture looks like.

## The Leaderless Encirclement

The moves from above are sweeping. The moves from below are almost invisible.

MIT's Project NANDA 2025 report revealed a striking fact: only 40% of companies had purchased official LLM subscriptions, yet employees at over 90% of companies were regularly using personal AI tools for work [9]. TELUS Digital's survey was more specific — 68% of employees using AI accessed ChatGPT, Copilot, or Gemini through personal accounts, and 57% admitted to entering sensitive company information [10].

35% of employees paid out of pocket for AI tools [1]. Not company-issued, not reimbursed — paid for themselves. Because 75% of users reported that AI genuinely improved their work speed or quality [21]. Productivity gains are a hard need; they don't wait for approval.

Even more ironic: 85% of people who had company-approved AI tools were still using unapproved ones [11]. The company-provided tools weren't good enough, fast enough, or had too many restrictions. Employees voted with their feet.

This wasn't an organized movement. Nobody was rallying in group chats saying "let's all secretly use AI." Wharton professor Ethan Mollick coined a name for these people — "secret cyborgs" [12]. They kept all their techniques hidden, spoke normally in meetings during the day, and had ChatGPT help write tomorrow's report at night.

Why hide? Because 53% of people using AI worried it would make them look "replaceable" [13]. Finished three days' worth of work with AI? Don't mention it — mentioning it proves the position doesn't need three days. Gong CEO Amit Bendov put it precisely: "The real risk isn't that people are using AI — it's that they're pretending they're not" [14].

Only 39% of employees using AI had received any company training [11]. Most were self-taught. 37% of employees didn't use AI even when they could — not because they didn't want to, but because their colleagues weren't using it and they didn't dare go first [15].

Frontline AI adoption has no organization, no slogans — it's just each person's choice at their own desk. The motivation is as simple as it gets: getting off work earlier tonight.

This is the lower jaw. You don't even know when it's already closed.

## Those Who Can't Draw Their Swords

The upper jaw is the CEO's layoff numbers. The lower jaw is frontline Shadow AI. Caught in between: middle management.

What is the core value of middle management? Information filtering, upward reporting, project coordination, bridging top and bottom. Examine each one: upward reporting — AI can generate directly from data; information filtering — AI can handle it directly; project coordination — AI agents are encroaching on this domain; bridging communication — when executives can use AI to analyze frontline data directly, and frontline workers can use AI to understand executive strategy directly, where is the middleman's value?

The data speaks louder than any argument. Gartner predicts that by 2026, one in five organizations will use AI to eliminate at least half of their management layers [20]. Amazon's 14,000 management cuts represented approximately 13% of its global management workforce, with the goal of increasing the individual contributor-to-manager ratio by 15% — saving an estimated $3.6 billion annually [5]. TCS laid off 12,000 people, primarily targeting mid-to-senior management [16].

Middle management's predicament is a paradox. They're asked to prove upward that the AI strategy is landing — which means proving that their own layer is being effectively replaced. They're asked to push AI tool adoption downward — which means accelerating the erosion of their own value. Companies deploy black-box algorithms requiring intensive oversight while firing the overseers.

SHRM put it well: "Middle management isn't just a structural layer — it's the connective tissue of the organization" [17]. But the value of connective tissue is precisely what's easiest to overlook. You don't notice ligaments until they tear. 34% of organizations already anticipate professional compliance skill shortages [17] — the first wave of backlash from over-cutting middle management.

Middle managers aren't without resistance. But their resistance is covert. Nobody stands up at an all-hands meeting to say "I oppose AI." Their language is always delay tactics:

"I'm not against AI, I'm against using AI irresponsibly."

"AI is great, but we need more governance."

"The timing isn't right yet."

These aren't discussions — they're survival instincts. The pincer movement is lethal to middle management precisely because of this: you can't openly admit you're the one being threatened. Admitting it means admitting the position itself is the problem.

## Every Layer Is Cannae

If the pincer movement only happened at the "executive-middle-frontline" scale, it would be nothing more than an interesting observation. But reality is far more complex.

The pincer movement is fractal. Any node with a decision-making layer above and an execution layer below will get squeezed.

At the department level: the VP sets the AI roadmap, frontline developers are already having AI write their code — the department manager gets squeezed. At the team level: the Tech Lead mandates AI for code reviews, junior engineers are having AI generate entire modules — the Senior Engineer gets squeezed. Think about it: when Nadella says 20–30% of Microsoft's code is AI-written [4], those Seniors who spent a decade building their code review authority suddenly find that more and more of the code they review is AI-generated, and newcomers using AI are producing faster than they can write by hand. The moat of experience is being filled in. At the individual level: your boss demands you use AI to boost efficiency, your direct reports are already using AI to produce what you haven't done yet — the one getting squeezed is you.

The pincer movement is a structural mechanic. As long as hierarchy exists, it will inevitably occur. It doesn't need anyone to launch it. CEOs push AI because not pushing means the board replaces them. Frontline employees use ChatGPT because it means leaving work earlier tonight. Everyone chases their own local interest, and those local interests converge into a systematic strangling of the middle layer.

This is an emergent phenomenon. Just like AI's own emergence — nobody wrote "learn to reason" into GPT's training objective, but when scale grew large enough, reasoning ability grew on its own. Similarly, nobody designed the pincer movement's battle plan, but when every person at every level chases their own interest, the pincer grows on its own.

75% of executives believe their company has successfully adopted AI; only 45% of employees agree [1]. 55% of employers regret AI-driven layoffs [18]. Even those launching the upper jaw haven't thought it through. Forrester predicts that half of AI-attributed layoffs will be quietly rehired, but shifted offshore or at lower salaries [18]. Stanford's research found that 41% of AI implementations fell into the "unwanted or impossible" zone [19] — companies were automating tasks employees wanted to keep, while ignoring the tasks employees actually wanted automated.

Nobody is in control of this offensive.

---

## After the Jaws Close

The pincer movement has a few possible endings.

The first: full encirclement. Organizational structures flatten completely; middle management as a category disappears. Amazon is already doing this, aiming to "reduce bureaucracy and remove organizational layers" [5]. If AI can truly replace "coordination, reporting, and decision-making functions," then between the CEO and frontline executors, not many intermediate nodes are needed.

The second: redefinition. Middle management doesn't disappear, but its value shifts from "information processing" to "judgment, political navigation, interpersonal connection" — capabilities AI cannot yet replace. SHRM's "connective tissue" metaphor points in this direction [17]: there will always be things that need humans, but those things are no longer summarizing data and relaying directives.

The third: backlash. 55% of employers regret it [18]; 34% of organizations anticipate compliance skill shortages [17]. After over-cutting middle management, they discover governance gaps and oversight vacuums. Then they quietly rehire, just with different titles and lower pay.

Which will happen? Most likely all three simultaneously, at different companies, different departments, different times. Because this offensive itself was never under unified command or planning.

But you probably think this is someone else's problem. You're not a CEO — you don't need to lay people off. You're not middle management — you're not worried about getting squeezed.

Think again: Is your boss pushing AI? Are your subordinates or juniors already using AI to do what you're still doing by hand?

You're very likely part of both the upper and lower jaws simultaneously. Discussing AI governance frameworks in meetings during the day, secretly using ChatGPT to write tomorrow's report at night.

This offensive was launched by no one, and no one can call it off. Everyone chases their own local interest — CEOs want market cap, employees want efficiency, boards want a growth narrative. The systemic force created by these converging interests leaves you no counterparty to negotiate with.

Can you hold your ground? That question itself is wrong. You're already inside.
